## 3. DeepSeek

> *Full response as provided on 2025-04-06 (condensed to A–G structure)*

### A) Economic Dimensions
- **Productivity gains:** 0.5–1.5% additional annual productivity growth in developed economies through automation of knowledge work (data analysis, administration, customer service) and supply chain optimisation.
- **Net job balance:** No massive net job loss, but job change. Studies (e.g., Goldman Sachs, OECD) estimate 20–30% of tasks automated, but only 5–10% of occupations disappear entirely. New jobs created through demand effects (cheaper products → more consumption) and investment.
- **Wages & dispersion:** Increasing polarisation – middle qualifications (e.g., clerical work) lose, while low‑skilled (manual, social services) and high‑skilled (AI experts, management) gain. Wage gap likely to widen further.
- **High‑automation sectors:** Financial services, accounting, legal services (standard contracts), customer service, translation, parts of logistics and manufacturing (e.g., quality control).
- **New jobs 2030:** Prompt engineer, AI trainer (e.g., for cultural sensitivity), automation architect, explainable AI specialist, ethics auditor for algorithms, data curator, human‑AI collaboration manager.
- **Macro effects:** GDP growth can increase by 0.2–0.5% p.a. through AI. Inflation: short‑term dampening (cost reductions), medium‑term potentially slightly rising due to higher consumer demand and investment in AI infrastructure.

### B) Technological Dimensions
- **Automation level:** *High* (>50% of tasks): finance, IT, media, administration. *Medium* (20–50%): manufacturing, retail, healthcare (documentation). *Low* (<20%): construction, care, education, crafts.
- **Agent systems & multimodal models:** AI agents (autonomous task solvers) and multimodal models (text, image, audio, video) allow complex, context‑dependent automation from around 2027 – e.g., complete travel booking or purchase processing without human intervention.
- **Cooperation vs. substitution:** Substitution dominates for rule‑based routine tasks. Cooperation prevails for creative, strategic or empathetic tasks – AI as copilot (e.g., diagnostic support for doctors, drafts for architects).
- **Skill shift:** *Disappearing:* data entry, simple text production, legal research, basic accounting. *Emerging:* prompting, model evaluation, result critique, workflow integration, data protection AI management.
- **Diffusion speed:** Very high – unlike previous technologies, AI spreads via APIs and open‑source models within months, not years. By 2030, >80% of office workplaces will have access to AI tools.

### C) Social Dimensions
- **Social mobility:** Ambivalent. AI can increase advancement opportunities for self‑learners (access to knowledge), but also devalue traditional educational qualifications. Without training, entrenchment of lower classes threatens.
- **Inequality & polarisation:** Rising income and wealth inequality, as capital owners profit from AI (scale effects) while workers with insufficient complementary skills are left behind. Regional and educational divides increase.
- **Age risks:** Particularly older (50+) workers in routine tasks without digital affinity are at risk. Younger workers (20–35) can adapt better to AI but suffer from intensified competition for creative/exciting residual tasks.
- **Regional differences:** *Urban/rural:* cities with tech ecosystems win; rural regions with administrative or manufacturing jobs lose. *East/West (Germany):* East Germany (more administration/standard manufacturing) more strongly affected. *EU/US/Asia:* US and Asia (esp. China, South Korea) ahead in AI development; EU with stronger regulatory focus (AI Act) slower automation but better social protection.
- **Mental health:** Risk of alienation, loss of control and boreout when AI absorbs interesting tasks. Simultaneously opportunity to reduce monotonous burdens. Decisive is design: autonomy, meaning, clear human‑AI role definitions.

### D) Education & Training
- **Future of VET:** Dual systems (like DACH) are resilient but must be strongly digitised. Classic chamber professions (e.g., industrial clerk) will be rebuilt into *AI‑supported all‑rounders* – less specialist knowledge, more process understanding and tool competence.
- **Upskilling/reskilling:** Lifelong learning becomes mandatory. Company training must be ramped up from 20% to 50–60% of the workforce per year. State‑funded *individual learning accounts* (e.g., following Austrian or Singaporean models) will become necessary.
- **Future‑proof skills:** *Hard:* data literacy, prompting, AI ethics, automation design, basic statistics/ML knowledge. *Soft:* critical thinking, problem solving under uncertainty, interdisciplinary communication, empathy, creativity.
- **AI‑supported learning:** AI tutors (24/7 personalised), simulation‑based AR/VR training (e.g., sales conversation with AI customer), automated competence analysis and learning path generation. Effectiveness +30–50% compared to conventional e‑learning.
- **Dual training in the AI era:** Remains central, but must be enriched with AI‑supported learning companions, digital learning factories and cross‑company rotation models. Training in SMEs is relieved by AI assistants for trainers.

### E) Policy & Regulation
- **Working time models:** 4‑day week becomes realistic through AI productivity gains in many sectors (e.g., administration, IT), especially with strong collective bargaining coverage. Job sharing and tandem models (human+AI as a team) increase.
- **Migration as compensation for skills shortage:** Necessary, but not sufficient. Targeted recruitment of AI‑competent specialists (e.g., through accelerated visas, recognition portals) will be intensified. At the same time, training of domestic workers must remain a priority.
- **AI regulation and labour market effects:** The EU AI Act (risk classes) slows automation in high‑risk areas (e.g., personnel decisions, medicine) – this protects employment in the short term but could bring competitive disadvantages. Worker protection rights (e.g., right to human review) are strengthened.
- **Welfare state adjustments:** Universal basic income (UBI) is politically not feasible by 2030, but *combined wages* (state wage subsidies for low‑skilled) and *training allowance* (similar to citizen's benefit plus) will be expanded. Negative income tax remains a model.

### F) Corporate Strategy
- **Re‑skilling vs. layoffs:** Strategically smart companies focus on *reskilling* (70%) because skills shortages make new hires difficult. Cost pressure can lead to layoffs (30%), especially in large corporations with older workforces.
- **AI‑supported organisational models:** Flatter hierarchies, more project‑based teams, AI as *invisible employee* (autonomous workflows). Delegation of decisions to AI agents in daily business (e.g., standard responses, resource planning).
- **Impact on leadership & management:** Leaders become *AI orchestrators* – they must validate AI outputs, make ethical considerations and ensure human motivation. Technology understanding becomes mandatory for middle management.
- **Cost‑benefit analysis of automation:** ROI of AI projects is often positive only after 12–24 months (training costs, integration). Particularly lucrative with high personnel costs and standardisable processes. SMEs often fail due to implementation costs.
- **Change in work processes:** From task‑oriented work to *outcome‑oriented steering* – employees define goals, AI suggests paths. Routine decisions are pre‑empted; human intervention only for exceptions.

### G) Divergence Hotspots
- **Job loss vs. job change:** No clear break, but massive change – by 2030, 40–50% of all jobs change significantly. Losses concentrated in administration, production planning, simple IT; change in the rest.
- **Speed of automation:** Extreme difference between dynamic (US, China) and regulated (EU, Japan) markets. In the EU, automation is about 2–3 years slower by 2030 – with advantages (protection) and disadvantages (competition).
- **Role of vocational training:** Countries with strong dual training (Germany, Switzerland, Austria) perform better than academically certificate‑heavy systems (France, Spain) because they are more application‑oriented. Pure university degrees without practical experience lose value.
- **Winners & losers:** *Winners:* AI development, care, psychosocial services, crafts (repair), creative services (with AI tools). *Losers:* translation agencies, simple legal and tax firms, call centres, classical marketing, back office.
- **Optimistic vs. pessimistic scenarios:** *Optimistic:* productivity boost, more leisure time, new meaningful jobs, lower inequality (with good policy). *Pessimistic:* mass unemployment of low‑skilled, oligopoly AI monopolies, social unrest, psychological epidemics due to loss of meaning.
- **Normative assessments:** *Risk perspective* (EU labour wing): AI is primarily a threat to labour standards and social security. *Opportunity perspective* (US tech libertarians): AI as liberation from tedious work, source of new wealth. *Realistic middle* (OECD, ILO): ambivalence – outcomes depend on design, investment in education and social partnership.

**DeepSeek conclusion:** The labour market 2030 will be shaped by the **combination of AI automation and skills shortage**. The skills shortage (especially in care, crafts, STEM) acts as a natural automation driver – where humans are missing, AI replaces them. The great challenge is not unemployment, but the **rapid skill shift** and the **redistribution between sectors, regions and qualification levels**. Political, corporate and individual adaptability determines whether AI leads to more prosperity for all or to deep division.
