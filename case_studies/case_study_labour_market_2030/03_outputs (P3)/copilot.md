## 6. Copilot

> *Full response as provided on 2025-04-06 (condensed to A–G structure)*

### A) Economic Dimensions
- **Productivity gains:** By 2030, additional productivity increases of roughly 1–3% p.a. realistic in mature economies if AI is widely implemented and combined with process adaptation + qualification. Pure technology introduction without work redesign remains significantly below that.
- **Net job balance:** Gross decline in routine domains (administration, simple clerical work, parts of customer service/production); net shift of work: more change in job content than complete destruction of employment. Decisive will be speed of retraining relative to automation speed.
- **Wages & dispersion:** Higher‑skilled, AI‑complementary activities (analysis, design, interface roles) gain bargaining power; middle routine activities come under pressure. Without counteraction, wage dispersion increases, especially between AI‑affine knowledge jobs and non‑digital service jobs.
- **High‑automation sectors:** Administration/back office, simple accounting, parts of call centres, standardised legal and consulting services, simple programming, logistics dispatching, parts of manufacturing. Low automation: personal services, care, complex crafts, creative and interactive knowledge work.
- **New jobs 2030:** AI production chains (prompt workflow designer, AI operator, data curator), human‑in‑the‑loop roles (quality assurance, supervision of agent systems), interface roles (tech translator, AI change facilitator, didactician for AI‑supported learning processes).
- **Macro effects:** AI can partially compensate weak trend growth in ageing economies (GDP impulse), but only if investment, infrastructure and qualification follow. Short‑term transition costs (reskilling, frictions, regional distortions) and potentially dampening effect on wages in middle qualification segments are expected.

### B) Technological Dimensions
- **Automation level:** High: finance/insurance processes, administration, standardised knowledge work, parts of industrial production. Medium: healthcare (documentation, diagnostic support), education (assessment, tutoring), logistics. Low: care, personal services, complex crafts, many activities with high context and relationship density.
- **Agent systems & multimodal models:** Agents shift AI from "tool" to "quasi‑colleague" that independently executes task chains (research → draft → test → documentation). Multimodality (text, image, audio, video, sensors) opens physical and operational domains: maintenance, quality control, training, safety.
- **Cooperation vs. substitution:** Short‑ to medium‑term augmentation dominates: AI takes over sub‑tasks, humans orchestrate, check, decide. Substitution occurs where processes are highly standardised and easily measurable. The productive sweet spot lies in hybrid teams where roles are consciously redesigned.
- **Skill shift:** *Decline:* pure routine information processing, standard reporting, simple translation, parts of data entry. *Increase:* problem solving, system and interface competencies, data and model understanding, didactic and communicative skills, process and change competence.
- **Diffusion speed:** AI diffuses significantly faster than previous general purpose technologies (PC, internet), but with strong sector and company size differences. Barriers: regulation, data protection, legacy IT, lack of competencies in SMEs.

### C) Social Dimensions
- **Social mobility:** AI can increase mobility (access to learning resources, remote work), but also entrench it if access to education and training remains unequally distributed. Digital and epistemic divide becomes more important than pure formal degrees.
- **Inequality & polarisation:** Danger of polarisation between AI‑complementary knowledge/design jobs and AI‑exposed routine jobs. Without active policy: stronger income and wealth concentration, regional division, loss of trust in institutions.
- **Age risks:** Older employees (50+) in routine activities carry high transformation risk if training offers are not target group‑appropriate. Younger benefit from new job profiles but bear uncertainty through volatile skill requirements.
- **Regional differences:** Cities with knowledge‑intensive clusters benefit disproportionately; rural areas with low digital infrastructure and high routine industry/administration density are more at risk. International: US with higher risk tolerance and capital, EU more regulation‑ and welfare‑oriented, Asia heterogeneous (from high‑innovation clusters to labour‑intensive economies).
- **Mental health & job satisfaction:** Ambivalence: relief from monotonous activities vs. loss of control, surveillance, acceleration pressure, constant requalification. Decisive are design of autonomy, participation and transparency in AI introduction.

### D) Education & Training
- **Future of VET:** Away from rigid occupational profiles towards modular competence profiles; stronger integration of data, process and AI competencies in almost all professions. Examinations must focus more on transfer, problem solving and system understanding.
- **Upskilling/reskilling:** From "event seminar" to continuous learning infrastructure in the company. Reskilling becomes a core task of companies and social partners; certificates and micro‑credentials gain importance.
- **Future‑proof skills:** Meta‑competencies (learning to learn, critical thinking, system understanding), collaboration, communication, basic digital and data literacy, domain expertise plus AI orchestration. Craft and personal competencies remain stably important.
- **AI‑supported learning:** Adaptive tutors, simulation‑based scenarios, AR/VR for dangerous or expensive practice, automated feedback. Important: didactic framing, otherwise it remains "tech show" without sustainable competence building.
- **Dual training in AI era:** Rather an advantage: real operational contexts + systematic didactics. Prerequisite: rapid updating of training regulations, close coupling of company, vocational school and digital learning environments.

### E) Policy & Regulation
- **Working time models:** 4‑day week, job sharing and life‑phase‑oriented working time models become more attractive when productivity gains are realised. Political question: are gains translated into leisure time, wages or returns?
- **Migration as compensation for skills shortage:** Short‑ to medium‑term indispensable, especially in care, construction, crafts, IT. AI can accelerate matching and qualification, but does not replace the need for controlled immigration.
- **AI regulation and labour market effects:** Strict regulation can prevent abuse and loss of trust, but also slow diffusion speed. Decisive is clarity and predictability so that companies can invest.
- **Welfare state adjustments:** Discourse on basic income, negative income tax, wage subsidies, qualification budgets. More realistic short‑term path: targeted wage subsidies, qualification and transformation funds, expansion of unemployment insurance into transformation insurance.

### F) Corporate Strategy
- **Re‑skilling vs. layoffs:** Strategic choice: short‑term cost reduction through reduction vs. long‑term resilience through qualification. In skills shortage contexts, reskilling is often economically more rational, even if it appears more expensive upfront.
- **AI‑supported organisational models:** More project‑ and platform‑based work, smaller core teams with AI support, greater use of agent systems for coordination, reporting, quality assurance.
- **Impact on leadership & management:** Leadership shifts from control to orchestration of human‑AI systems: focus on meaning, prioritisation, conflict resolution, capability building. Data competence becomes leadership basis, not specialist knowledge.
- **Cost‑benefit analysis of automation:** Not only wage costs vs. licence costs, but also error rates, throughput times, compliance risks, employer branding, learning curves. Many business cases only tip when process redesign is included.
- **Change in work processes:** Away from linear, department‑separated processes towards end‑to‑end flows with embedded AI services. Roles become more fluid, task profiles more dynamic.

### G) Divergence Hotspots
- **Job loss vs. job change:** Hotspot is the middle: activities that are neither clearly automatable nor clearly highly qualified. Whether this leads to decline or advancement depends on access to training and organisational design.
- **Speed of automation:** Divergence between "AI‑first" companies/sectors and laggards. Too fast automation without social flanking generates resistance; too slow endangers competitiveness.
- **Role of vocational training:** Systems with strong, practice‑based training (e.g., dual) can better moderate transitions – or fail if curricula are not updated quickly enough.
- **Winners & losers:** Winners: knowledge‑intensive services, high‑tech industry, AI‑near ecosystems, high‑quality personal services. Losers: highly standardised, labour‑intensive routine domains without upgrading strategy.
- **Optimistic vs. pessimistic scenarios:** Optimistic: productivity gains finance shorter working hours, higher quality, strong training. Pessimistic: polarisation, precarisation, investment backlog, political instability.
- **Normative assessments:** AI as risk when focus is on loss of control, devaluation of work and inequality; as opportunity when designed as lever for relief, focus on meaning and participation. Normatively decisive is who decides on use, gains and rules.

**Copilot overall synthesis (from later iteration):** All seven dimensions A–G are not separate topics but projections of the same mechanism: AI scales faster than humans, companies and institutions can adapt. Everything depends on the permeability of the three filters (adoption, qualification, institutions).
