# 06 – Power Layer

This file adds the **power dimension** to the TNS analysis. The three filters (adoption, qualification, institutions) are not neutral. They operate within structures of **access, control, profit distribution, and risk concentration**.

For each major claim, we ask:

1. **Who controls the technology?** (Ownership, infrastructure, standards)
2. **Who has access?** (Cost, education, geography, demography)
3. **Who profits?** (Income, productivity gains, capital returns)
4. **Who bears the risks?** (Job loss, skill obsolescence, health, exclusion)

The power layer is **not an additional block** – it is a **meta‑filter** applied to every synthesis conclusion.

---

## 1. Power Layer Applied to Key Claims

### Claim A: Productivity gains from AI

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **Control** | Large tech firms (OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Anthropic, Chinese players) control foundational models. Open‑source models exist but lag in performance. |
| **Access** | Unequal – large corporations and tech‑affine sectors (IT, finance) have early access; SMEs, public sector, rural areas lag. Educational access is income‑dependent. |
| **Profit** | Productivity gains flow primarily to capital owners (shareholders, founders) and high‑skilled AI‑complementary workers. Wage share of GDP tends to fall. |
| **Risk** | Workers in automatable routine tasks bear displacement risk; mid‑skilled administrative workers are most exposed. |

**Power insight:** Without redistribution mechanisms, the Solow paradox (high investment, low measurable productivity) is accompanied by **increasing inequality** – even if aggregate productivity rises.

---

### Claim D: Dual training as advantage (conditional)

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **Control** | Curricula controlled by chambers (IHK), ministries, and social partners – a slow, consensus‑driven process. Large firms have disproportionate influence. |
| **Access** | Open in principle, but quality varies by region and company size. Rural SMEs often lack resources for modern AI training infrastructure. |
| **Profit** | Large companies with dedicated training departments benefit most. SMEs struggle to keep up, risking a **two‑speed training landscape**. |
| **Risk** | Young people in regions with weak training infrastructure risk being trained for obsolete tasks. The “trap” scenario (Claude, DeepSeek) falls on them. |

**Power insight:** The dual system’s strength (stability, social partnership) is also its weakness (inertia, incumbent capture). Power asymmetry between large and small firms shapes who benefits.

---

### Claim E: AI regulation (EU AI Act)

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **Control** | Regulatory power lies with the EU Commission and member states. US and Chinese firms are subject to it only when operating in the EU market. |
| **Access** | Strict regulation raises compliance costs. Large incumbents have legal departments to manage this; start‑ups and SMEs are disproportionately burdened. |
| **Profit** | Established players (e.g., SAP, Siemens) can use regulation as a moat against agile competitors. US firms may delay EU market entry, reducing consumer choice. |
| **Risk** | Over‑regulation risks **innovation offshoring** – AI development moves to less regulated jurisdictions (US, China, Gulf states). European workers lose future jobs. |

**Power insight:** The “brake vs. protection” debate is not just normative – it is a **struggle over who gets to set the rules** and who can afford to comply.

---

### Claim G: Speed of adaptation determines outcome

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| **Control** | Adaptation speed is not neutral. Incumbents with resources can slow change to protect their position; fast‑moving challengers (start‑ups, tech firms) push for disruption. |
| **Access** | Regions with existing tech clusters (Munich, Berlin, Zurich) adapt faster; peripheral regions lack the infrastructure and human capital to keep pace. |
| **Profit** | First‑movers (early adopters of AI) capture market share and productivity gains. Late‑movers risk obsolescence. |
| **Risk** | Workers in slow‑adapting regions and sectors bear the cost of structural change without the benefits. The “patchwork transformation” (ChatGPT) creates winners and losers by geography. |

**Power insight:** The master question – *does technology scale faster than social systems can adapt?* – is also a power question. Those who control scaling (big tech) and those who control adaptation (governments, incumbents) are often in conflict.

---

## 2. Power Layer as Fourth Filter (Meta‑Filter)

The three original filters are now **nested** within the power layer:

| Original Filter | Power Question Added |
|----------------|----------------------|
| **Adoption (firms)** | *Which firms can afford to adopt? Who is left out?* |
| **Qualification (individuals)** | *Who has access to training? Who designs the curricula?* |
| **Institution (state/IHK)** | *Who influences regulation? Whose interests are represented?* |

**Rule for future TNS applications:**  
Every synthesis statement must include a **power‑check paragraph** answering the four questions. If the answer is “unknown”, that gap must be documented as a limitation.

---

## 3. Power‑Sensitive Recommendations (for Policy & Strategy)

Based on the power layer analysis, we add the following **normative recommendations** (marked clearly as value‑based):

1. **To avoid a two‑speed society:** Public investment in AI infrastructure and training for SMEs, rural areas, and older workers.
2. **To prevent incumbent capture:** Open standards, interoperable systems, and anti‑monopoly measures in AI markets.
3. **To distribute gains:** Tax mechanisms (e.g., on AI‑generated capital income) to fund reskilling and social buffers.
4. **To reduce risk concentration:** Worker participation in AI deployment decisions (co‑determination, works councils).

*These recommendations are derived from the power layer analysis, not from model consensus. They reflect the operator’s normative position (see `07_reflection.md`).*

---

## 4. Limitations of the Power Layer

- **Data gaps:** No systematic data on “who controls AI” at the firm level; reliance on structural observation.
- **Causality:** Power effects are often indirect and hard to isolate from other factors.
- **Normative risk:** The power layer can become a vehicle for the operator’s political preferences if not documented transparently.

**Mitigation:** All power‑layer statements in this case study are **explicitly linked to observable asymmetries** (e.g., SME vs. large firm resources, urban vs. rural infrastructure). Where interpretation is required, it is marked as operator judgment.
